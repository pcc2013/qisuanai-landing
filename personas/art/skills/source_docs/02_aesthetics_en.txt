Aesthetics asks seemingly simple yet profoundly deep questions: What is beauty? Are standards of beauty objective or subjective? Plato believed beauty was an imitation of eternal Forms, making beauty a kind of truth. Kant separated beauty from truth and morality, defining aesthetic judgment as disinterested pleasure - beauty as a free activity of the mind. Hegel saw not just individual beautiful artworks but the entire history of art as the unfolding of Absolute Spirit. In the 20th century, Duchamp signed a urinal and submitted it to an exhibition, upending all assumptions that art must be handmade and beautiful. The most important skill aesthetics teaches us is not judging beauty but reflecting on the reasons behind our judgments.
